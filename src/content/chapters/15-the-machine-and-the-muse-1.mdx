<h1 class="chapter-title">The Machine and the Muse</h1>
<p class="chapter-subtitle">AI in the arts: ancient manuscripts, music composition, and what creativity means when machines can generate.</p>
<div class="chapter-title-rule"></div>
<div class="prose">

<p class="drop-cap">In 2021, a team of researchers at the University of Groningen published a result that had eluded scholars for three centuries. The Dead Sea Scrolls had long been known to contain two distinct writing styles in a single document — the Great Isaiah Scroll. The question of whether those styles represented two different scribes or one scribe with changing habits had never been resolved. An AI system analysed the handwriting patterns at the level of individual letter strokes — curvature, ink distribution, spatial positioning — and confirmed two scribes with 96 percent certainty, identifying precisely where one ended and the other began.</p>

The model did not read Hebrew. It did not understand the theological significance of the text. It detected patterns in images that were invisible to human eyes across three centuries of scholarship.

## The Akkadian Tablets

A similar breakthrough came the same year from a different direction. Trained on existing translations of Akkadian cuneiform — one of the world's oldest writing systems — a model could fill gaps in damaged tablets that had resisted interpretation for decades. The model's reconstructions were later verified against newly discovered fragments.

<StudyNote text="Akkadian cuneiform was used across the ancient Near East for over 3,000 years. Thousands of tablets remain untranslated, largely because the pool of expert translators is vanishingly small compared to the volume of material." />

<CalloutQuote text="AI did not replace the scholars. It multiplied what they could examine. A human expert can verify a reconstruction in minutes that would have taken months to produce manually." />

These applications share a structure: AI as pattern detector over domains where the pattern space is too vast for human review at scale. The same capability that allowed AlexNet to find cats in photographs allowed these systems to find scribal signatures in ancient handwriting.

## Music Without a Composer

<Accordion question="How does AI actually compose music?" id="accordion-music">
  Music generation models treat music as a sequence prediction problem — the same architecture that underlies language models. A Transformer model trained on MIDI files or audio recordings learns the statistical patterns of how notes, chords, rhythms, and dynamics follow each other. Given a prompt (a genre, a key, a melodic fragment, or a textual description), it generates continuations that are statistically consistent with the training data. The model doesn't understand melody or harmony the way a musician does — it has learned which patterns tend to appear together in its training set. The output can be remarkably coherent at the phrase level but often lacks long-range structural development — the qualities that make a composition feel purposeful rather than merely plausible.
</Accordion>

In 2023, an AI-assisted track by the Beatles — a vocal fragment by John Lennon processed and extended by machine learning — reached number one in multiple countries. The technology was not controversial because it created something alien. It was controversial because it created something credible.

The more interesting question is not whether AI can generate plausible music, but how human musicians use these tools. Composers report using generative models the way writers use word processors: as an environment for exploration, not a replacement for judgment. The model generates options; the composer selects, shapes, and discards.

## What Creativity Actually Requires

The arrival of generative AI in creative fields has forced a clarification of what creativity consists of. Three claims circulate.

The first: creativity is recombination of existing elements, and AI is better at this than humans. The second: creativity requires intentionality, and AI has none. The third: what matters is not the process but the output, and if the output achieves its intended effect, the process is irrelevant.

<CalloutQuote text="None of these claims is obviously right. The interesting observation is that the debate forces us to articulate what we value about creativity — and why." />

For scholars of ancient languages, the question is irrelevant. A model that reconstructs a damaged tablet accurately is useful regardless of whether the reconstruction counts as creative. For musicians, the question matters enormously. For audiences, it may depend entirely on whether they know.

## The Digital Humanities

Beyond music and visual art, AI is reshaping the humanities more broadly. Historians use natural language processing to analyse millions of documents and identify patterns across centuries of correspondence. Literary scholars use computational methods to trace the evolution of style and theme across large corpora. Archaeologists use computer vision to classify ceramic fragments.

These applications are generating genuine discoveries — findings that would not have been possible without the scale that machine learning enables. They are also generating debates about what constitutes evidence, what counts as interpretation, and who owns the analytical process.

The pattern is familiar from medicine and law: AI amplifies what is possible, concentrates benefits where data is abundant, and raises questions that are not technical but normative. What should a reconstruction preserve? What should a composition feel like? What does it mean to understand a text?
